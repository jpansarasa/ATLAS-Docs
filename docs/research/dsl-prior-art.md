# DSL Extraction — Prior Art Survey

**Scope:** transformer-era literature (2022+) on three mechanistic hypotheses underlying the ATLAS DSL extraction approach. RNN/LSTM/DNN-era work (CopyNet, classical pointer networks, BiLSTM-CRF) is excluded as not directly applicable to transformer architectures.

**Companion to:** `ATLAS-DSL-PoC-Plan.md`

---

## TL;DR

- **Mechanism (a) — structured output preserves extraction fidelity:** Empirically well-supported for *extraction-style* tasks (classification, NER, schema-filling, function calls) by GoLLIE (ICLR 2024), CodeIE / Code4Struct / Code4UIE (ACL 2023), the BFCL leaderboard (Patil et al., ICML 2025), and structured-generation benchmarks from the Outlines team. The principal counterevidence is Tam et al.'s "Let Me Speak Freely?" (EMNLP Industry 2024), which shows JSON-mode degrades *reasoning* but improves *classification*. The two findings are reconcilable and both apply to the ATLAS pipeline.
- **Mechanism (b) — span-copying beats paraphrasing for extraction fidelity:** Indirectly but strongly supported. Maynez et al. 2020 established that hallucinations concentrate on entities and quantities; FActScore (Min et al., EMNLP 2023), GopherCite (Menick et al. 2022), and Attributed QA (Bohnet et al. 2022) all show that explicit quote-grounded or atomic-span outputs are measurably more faithful than free-form prose; "Benford's Curse" (Shao et al. 2025) provides mechanistic evidence that LLMs hallucinate digits in particular. Olsson et al.'s induction-heads work (Anthropic 2022) gives a plausible circuit-level basis for "copy mode."
- **Mechanism (c) — structured/compressed/symbolic CoT can match or beat natural-language CoT:** Strongly supported on numeric and symbolic tasks by Program-of-Thoughts (Chen et al., TMLR 2023, ~12% over CoT on average), PAL (Gao et al., ICML 2023, +40% on GSM-hard), Chain-of-Code (Li et al., DeepMind 2023, +12% on BIG-Bench Hard), Chain-of-Symbol (Hu et al., COLM 2024, up to +60.8% on Brick World), Faithful CoT (Lyu et al., IJCNLP-AACL 2023), Chain-of-Draft (Xu et al. 2025, 7.6% of CoT tokens at comparable accuracy), and Sketch-of-Thought (Aytes et al., EMNLP 2025, up to 78% token reduction on 15 reasoning datasets).

**Bottom line:** The R2/R3 jump from 19% to 91.7% numeric recall on qwen3:30b is consistent with — and at the upper range of — what the published literature reports when (i) extraction is recast as schema-filling rather than prose, (ii) the model is encouraged to copy verbatim from context rather than regenerate, and (iii) reasoning is offloaded to a structured representation. The single mechanism that is least directly supported in the peer-reviewed literature is "copy-mode vs paraphrase-mode" as a clean controlled contrast for *numeric* extraction — the ATLAS results may themselves be a contribution there.

## Key Findings

1. **Structured output and extraction are a natural match; reasoning tasks are a different story.** Tam et al. (EMNLP Industry 2024) is the most-cited "structured output hurts" paper, but a careful read shows that on classification tasks (DDXPlus, Sports, Task280, Multifin in their Figure 4) structured formats *helped* across most of the five models tested, while strict JSON-mode hurt multi-step arithmetic. The mechanism they identify — JSON-mode forcing "answer" before "reasoning" — is a schema-design failure mode, not a structured-output failure mode. For an extraction pipeline that emits a schema of pre-existing facts from the context, this is the regime where the literature consistently shows structured output wins.
2. **The dottxt GSM8K result is from a non-peer-reviewed company blog**, but a peer-reviewed analog exists: GoLLIE (Sainz et al., ICLR 2024) reports zero-shot NER F1 jumping from 24 (GPT-3.5 with NL guidelines) on Music and 20 on Politics to 68 and 57 respectively when schemas are represented as Python classes — a 30–45 point absolute lift.
3. **Quote-grounded generation is the most rigorous evidence for span-copying.** GopherCite (DeepMind, 2022) achieves 80%/67% "high-quality and supported" answer rates on NaturalQuestions/ELI5 filtered subsets by *requiring* verbatim quotes; Bohnet et al. (Google, 2022) formalize this as Attributed QA and show retrieve-then-read beats closed-book paraphrastic generation under the AIS framework.
4. **Numbers are a special, well-documented failure mode.** Shao et al.'s "Benford's Curse" (2025) localizes digit-frequency bias to specific FFN neurons; Maynez et al. 2020 found extrinsic hallucinations cluster on named entities and quantities; FinDVer (EMNLP 2024) and FinTagging (2025) confirm this empirically for financial documents. **This is arguably the single strongest mechanistic explanation for the size of the numeric-recall jump.**
5. **Code/DSL as a reasoning scaffold is one of the strongest empirical findings of 2022–2025.** PoT, PAL, CoC, and Faithful-CoT collectively show 12–40 point gains over natural-language CoT on numeric and symbolic tasks. The mechanism the original authors attribute the gains to is identical to hypothesis (c): offloading precision-sensitive sub-tasks from token-by-token generation to a structurally constrained format.
6. **Induction heads (Olsson et al., Anthropic 2022)** provide circuit-level support for the "copy mode" hypothesis. The paper's central finding is that small attention-head circuits implement the algorithm `[A][B]…[A] → [B]` (copy the token that followed A last time) and that these circuits appear to scale up to most in-context learning behavior in large transformers.

## Details

### Mechanism (a) — Structured / DSL output preserves extraction fidelity

**Supporting evidence (transformer-era):**

- **Outlines / Efficient Guided Generation (Willard & Louf, arXiv:2307.09702, 2023).** Reformulates LM decoding as transitions over a finite-state machine, enabling regex- and CFG-constrained generation with O(1) average overhead per token. The .txt company's follow-up benchmarks claim structured generation gives consistent improvements across all eight models tested on GSM8K, with greater-than-10% lifts in seven of eight cases — though this is on a company blog, not peer-reviewed. Mechanism the authors propose: constraining the decoder to a valid grammar removes token-level format errors; "thought-control" (forcing intermediate fields to appear in the right order) lifts performance independently of the format constraint.
- **GoLLIE (Sainz et al., ICLR 2024, arXiv:2310.03668).** A Code-LLaMA fine-tuned to follow Python-class-with-docstring annotation guidelines for IE. Per Figure 1 of the paper: zero-shot F1 on Music is 24 for GPT-3.5-turbo with natural-language guidelines versus 68 for GoLLIE; on Politics, 20 vs 57. Outperforms prior SOTA on zero-shot IE across multiple benchmarks. **Mechanism the authors attribute the gain to:** representing the task as code "addresses several challenges typically associated with natural language instructions" — primarily that strong label priors in the LLM are forced to attend to schema details when those details are in the form of Python class definitions with docstrings.
- **CodeIE (Li et al., ACL 2023), Code4Struct (Wang, Li, Ji, ACL 2023), Code4UIE (Guo et al., 2023).** All three find that representing entities/events/relations as Python classes and asking a code-pretrained LLM to instantiate them outperforms natural-language IE prompts. Code4Struct shows that with only 50 training instances, code-style event-argument extraction matches a fully-supervised model trained on 4,202 instances, and beats prior SOTA by 20.8% absolute F1 under matched conditions.
- **Berkeley Function-Calling Leaderboard, V1–V4 (Patil et al., ICML 2025).** Evaluates serial/parallel function calls via Abstract Syntax Tree matching. Establishes that open-weight model capability has converged near the top: per the BFCL V1 public leaderboard, Llama-3.1-405B-Instruct leads at 88.5% and Llama-3.1-70B-Instruct at 84.8%. The .txt team's analysis on BFCL shows that *applying structured generation to open-weight models* can lift Meta-Llama-3-8B-Instruct from 58.73% to 84.25% on the leaderboard — a textbook example of structured output preserving extraction fidelity at constant model capability.
- **LLMStructBench (2025) and ExtractBench (2026).** Both benchmarks document that "even state-of-the-art models often fail to generate fully schema-compliant and semantically faithful JSON outputs" and identify that the failure mode is on long, deeply nested schemas with both syntactic and semantic errors. ExtractBench (PDF-to-JSON, 35 documents, 12,867 fields) finds that "valid JSON measures the fraction of extractions producing parseable, schema-conforming JSON — a necessary but insufficient condition for correctness." Implication for ATLAS: schema design matters as much as the constraint mechanism itself.
- **UniversalNER (Zhou et al., 2024), GLiNER (Zaratiana et al., NAACL 2024), GLiNER2 (2025).** Show that compact, bidirectional, *schema-aware* models can outperform fine-tuned LLMs on zero-shot NER while running on CPU — directly relevant to a CPU-based extraction pipeline.

**Counterevidence:**

- **"Let Me Speak Freely?" (Tam et al., EMNLP Industry 2024, arXiv:2408.02442).** Compares (i) constrained-decoding JSON mode, (ii) format-restricting instructions, (iii) NL-to-Format two-step, and (iv) free natural language across GSM8K, Last-Letter-Concat, Shuffled Objects (reasoning) and DDXPlus, Sports, Task280, Multifin (classification). Headline result: "we observe a significant decline in LLMs' reasoning abilities under format restrictions… stricter format constraints generally lead to greater performance degradation in reasoning tasks." However, their Figure 4 shows the opposite for classification: structured formats helped on most classification datasets across five models. **Mechanism the authors attribute the reasoning drop to:** JSON-mode forces the "answer" key to be emitted before the "reasoning" key, bypassing CoT entirely — a schema-design problem, not a structured-output problem. The .txt rebuttal notes the paper conflates "structured generation" with "JSON-mode" and uses different prompts for the structured vs unstructured conditions.
- **Instructor / Bad Schemas (useinstructor.com, 2024).** Empirical: "JSON mode is much more sensitive than Tool Calling" — changing a field name from `potential_final_choice` / `final_choice` to `potential_answers` / `final_answer` lifted accuracy from 4.5% to 95%. Engineering implication: the structured-output gain in extraction is real, but is brittle to schema design.

**Strongest single piece of evidence:** GoLLIE (Sainz et al., ICLR 2024). Peer-reviewed paper at a top venue, directly testing schema-as-code IE representation, with controlled comparisons and numeric magnitudes (e.g., 24→68 F1 on Music NER) that match the ATLAS empirical regime.

### Mechanism (b) — Span-copying / extractive generation beats paraphrastic generation for extraction tasks

**Supporting evidence (transformer-era):**

- **Maynez, Narayan, Bohnet & McDonald — "On Faithfulness and Factuality in Abstractive Summarization" (ACL 2020).** Pre-2022 but the canonical citation. Found that "intrinsic and extrinsic hallucinations happen frequently — in more than 70% of single-sentence summaries" across RNN, CNN, Transformer, and pretrained models on XSum. Critically, a large fraction of extrinsic hallucinations happen on named entities and quantities — exactly the spans the ATLAS pipeline must extract verbatim. Mechanism the authors propose: abstractive paraphrasing introduces an opportunity to substitute plausible-but-wrong content; the more the model rewrites, the more it hallucinates.
- **FActScore (Min et al., EMNLP 2023, arXiv:2305.14251).** "FACTSCORE, a new evaluation that breaks a generation into a series of atomic facts and computes the percentage of atomic facts supported by a reliable knowledge source… ChatGPT only achieves 58%" on biographies. The paper's key methodological claim is that "generations often contain a mixture of supported and unsupported pieces of information, making binary judgments of quality inadequate" — the explicit case for atomic-span outputs as the unit of evaluation. Mechanism: smaller, more verifiable units permit grounded checking.
- **GopherCite (Menick et al., DeepMind, arXiv:2203.11147, 2022).** A 280B-parameter LM trained via RL-from-human-preferences to answer with *verified verbatim quotes* from source documents. "GopherCite produces high quality (plausible and supported) answers 80% of the time when prompted with fact-seeking questions drawn from a filtered subset of NaturalQuestions… and 67% of the time on ELI5." Mechanism the authors propose: requiring the model to attach a verbatim span anchors its answer to retrievable evidence and enables abstention when no quote can be found.
- **Attributed Question Answering (Bohnet et al., Google, arXiv:2212.08037, 2022).** Formal framework (Attributable to Identified Sources, AIS) for measuring whether an answer is supported by a span. Empirically: "retrieve-then-read systems generate more complete attributions compared to LLM prompting." Mechanism: closed-book paraphrastic generation cannot be grounded; extractive pipelines can.
- **Lost in the Middle (Liu et al., TACL 2024, arXiv:2307.03172).** "Performance is often highest when relevant information occurs at the beginning or end of the input context, and significantly degrades when models must access relevant information in the middle of long contexts." Reported ~22-percentage-point gap on NaturalQuestions multi-doc QA at 2.3k tokens for GPT-3.5-Turbo. **Implication for ATLAS:** even when the LLM is in copy-mode, its ability to copy correctly depends on where the source span lives in the context. This argues for chunked extraction and explicit citation back to spans.
- **In-Context Learning and Induction Heads (Olsson et al., Anthropic, arXiv:2209.11895, 2022).** The mechanistic-interpretability foundation. "Induction heads are attention heads that implement a simple algorithm to complete token sequences like [A][B] … [A] → [B]." Six lines of evidence argue these heads "constitute the mechanism for the majority of all in-context learning." Mechanism for ATLAS: induction heads are *literally a copy mechanism* implemented in attention; a structured schema with verbatim slot-filling activates them more strongly than free-form paraphrastic generation, which gives the model latitude to drift toward learned distributional priors.
- **Benford's Curse (Shao, Lu & Yang, arXiv:2506.01734, 2025).** "Leading open-source LLMs show a consistent pattern of digit bias that resembles Benford's law… pruning these [FFN] neurons mitigates imbalanced overgeneration and partially corrects erroneous outputs." Mechanism: LLMs have a learned prior over digit frequencies inherited from pretraining; when asked to *regenerate* a number, they drift toward this prior; copying verbatim avoids it. **This is arguably the single most mechanistically satisfying explanation for the 19% → 91.7% numeric-recall jump.**
- **FinDVer (Zhao et al., EMNLP 2024, arXiv:2411.05764) and FinTagging (Wang et al., 2025).** Both find that locating and faithfully reproducing claim-relevant numerical data points from long, hybrid (table+text) financial documents is the dominant failure mode for current LLMs — domain-relevant for ATLAS.
- **Proof-Carrying Numbers protocol (Hakim et al., arXiv:2509.06902, 2025).** "Even when authoritative claims are retrieved, LLMs are unreliable at reproducing values faithfully… fabricated values may still appear alongside credible references, creating the illusion of fidelity." Treats numeric extraction as a presentation-layer problem requiring verbatim binding.

**Counterevidence / nuance:** None of the transformer-era papers surveyed offer a clean, controlled "copy mode vs paraphrase mode" head-to-head for numeric extraction. The closest negative would be the abstractive-summarization-can-beat-extractive literature on *fluency* metrics (ROUGE), but that literature consistently shows the abstractive gain comes at a faithfulness cost. The Tam et al. 2024 counter-result on reasoning tasks does not contradict this mechanism for extraction.

**Strongest single piece of evidence:** Shao et al. 2025 ("Benford's Curse") for the mechanism behind numeric drift, and Menick et al. 2022 (GopherCite) for the empirical demonstration that verbatim-quote-grounded generation reduces hallucination at scale.

### Mechanism (c) — Structured / compressed / symbolic CoT beats natural-language CoT

**Supporting evidence (transformer-era):**

- **Program-of-Thoughts (Chen, Ma, Wang & Cohen, TMLR 2023, arXiv:2211.12588).** "Under both few-shot and zero-shot settings, PoT can show an average performance gain over CoT by around 12% across all the evaluated datasets" on GSM8K, AQuA, SVAMP, TabMWP, MultiArith, FinQA, ConvFinQA, TATQA. Mechanism the authors propose: *disentangle* computation (delegated to a Python interpreter) from reasoning (handled by the LLM in code form). Especially strong on financial QA — directly relevant to ATLAS.
- **PAL — Program-Aided Language Models (Gao et al., ICML 2023, arXiv:2211.10435).** "On GSM-hard, a more challenging version of GSM that we create, PAL outperforms chain-of-thought by an absolute 40%." Specifically, with Codex, CoT degrades to roughly 20% while PAL holds at 61.2%. Mechanism: same disentanglement as PoT; the LLM emits interleaved NL comments and Python code; execution is offloaded.
- **Chain-of-Code (Li et al., Google DeepMind, arXiv:2312.04474, 2023).** "On BIG-Bench Hard, Chain of Code achieves 84%, a gain of 12% over Chain of Thought." Adds an "LMulator" — when code is non-executable (e.g., `detect_sarcasm()`), the LM simulates execution. Mechanism: code structure forces explicit handling of edge cases and provides a substrate where the LM can reason about both symbolic and semantic sub-tasks.
- **Chain-of-Symbol (Hu et al., COLM 2024, arXiv:2305.10276).** "The performance gain is strong, by up to 60.8% accuracy (from 31.8% to 92.6%) on Brick World scenarios for GPT-3.5-Turbo" while also reducing token use by 65.8%. Mechanism the authors propose: redundant natural language hampers LLMs on spatial tasks; symbolic compressed representations make the relevant structure salient.
- **Faithful Chain-of-Thought (Lyu et al., IJCNLP-AACL 2023, arXiv:2301.13379).** Two-stage framework: (i) translate NL query to a symbolic reasoning chain; (ii) execute with a deterministic solver. "With greedy decoding, Faithful CoT outperforms all baselines on 8 of the 10 datasets" with code-davinci-002. Mechanism: faithfulness — the reasoning chain *literally* produces the answer (it's executed), so it cannot be a post-hoc rationalization. Most directly relevant to the ATLAS verification flow (CPU extract → GPU verify).
- **Chain-of-Draft (Xu, Xie, Zhao & He, arXiv:2502.18600, 2025).** "Think step by step, but only keep a minimum draft for each thinking step, with 5 words at most." Reduces token usage to "just 7.6% of the tokens required by CoT" while maintaining comparable accuracy on arithmetic, commonsense, and symbolic reasoning with GPT-4o and Claude 3.5 Sonnet. Mechanism: short, structured drafts force the model to compress the reasoning state into high-information tokens, reducing both inference cost and the opportunity for drift.
- **Sketch-of-Thought (Aytes, Baek & Hwang, EMNLP 2025, arXiv:2503.05179).** The EMNLP-published version reports "token reductions of up to 78% with minimal accuracy loss" across 15 reasoning datasets (the arXiv preprint reports 84% across 18 datasets); the paper notes that "in tasks such as mathematical and multi-hop reasoning, it even improves accuracy while shortening outputs." Three paradigms — Conceptual Chaining, Chunked Symbolism, Expert Lexicons — routed by a lightweight DistilBERT-based classifier. Mechanism: route each task to a cognitively-appropriate compressed format.
- **Skeleton-of-Thought (Ning et al., 2023).** A different motivation (parallel decoding for latency) but illustrates that decomposing into a skeleton + parallel point-expansions is a useful structural prior.
- **Speculative CoT (Wang et al., arXiv:2504.19095, 2025), Draft-Thinking, DRAFT-RL.** Recent 2025 work treating draft reasoning as a learnable RL objective, showing that short-form structured drafts can outperform long natural-language CoT.

**Counterevidence / nuance:**

- **Tam et al. 2024 (already cited).** Strict JSON-mode degrades reasoning ~10–15%. But the mechanism the authors identify is that the schema forces "answer" before "reason." When the schema correctly puts reasoning first, structured CoT wins.
- **Chain of Draft for Software Engineering (Patel et al., arXiv:2506.10987, 2025).** Replicating CoD on SWE-Bench Lite found that aggressive compression hurts on long-horizon code tasks. Mechanism: compression budgets that work for arithmetic do not transfer to multi-file software reasoning.
- **Some 2025 papers note SoT and CoD "typically lag behind CoT in accuracy"** on harder reasoning. The pattern is: compressed/structured CoT wins on extraction-like and symbolic tasks; long natural-language CoT still wins on tasks where the chain itself is the deliverable (e.g., research-grade math proofs).

**Strongest single piece of evidence:** Faithful CoT (Lyu et al. 2023) for ATLAS, because it explicitly addresses the verification problem the GPU model is meant to solve — by making the reasoning chain executable, the verifier doesn't have to evaluate prose, it just runs the program.

## Synthesis

**Strongest empirical support: Mechanism (c).** The PoT → PAL → CoC → CoS → FCoT → CoD → SoT chain is a five-year arc of consistently positive results from major labs (CMU, DeepMind, Google, Anthropic-adjacent) showing that *structured or compressed CoT representations match or beat natural-language CoT* on the exact task categories that matter for IE: numeric reasoning, symbolic manipulation, schema-conformance, and financial QA. Token reductions of 70–90% with equal-or-better accuracy are now standard, with peer-reviewed magnitudes including PoT's +12% average over CoT, PAL's +40% on GSM-hard, CoC's +12% on BIG-Bench Hard, and CoS's +60.8% on Brick World.

**Most contested: Mechanism (a).** The "Let Me Speak Freely?" vs dottxt debate is unresolved in the public literature, but the resolution is mostly definitional: JSON-mode-with-bad-schema hurts; structured generation as a class helps, especially for extraction-shaped tasks. The ATLAS pipeline is squarely in the regime where the evidence is positive.

**Most under-evidenced: Mechanism (b)**, specifically for *numerical* extraction. There is strong indirect support — Maynez (entities/quantities are where hallucinations live), FActScore (atomic spans are more verifiable), Menick/Bohnet (quote-grounding reduces hallucination), Olsson (induction heads are literally a copy mechanism), Shao 2025 (digit-frequency bias is mechanistically traceable). But the literature lacks a controlled, peer-reviewed "copy-mode vs paraphrase-mode" benchmark on numeric extraction. **The 19% → 91.7% recall result on qwen3:30b is, on the evidence surveyed, in the range of the largest published structured-vs-unstructured gaps, and the numeric-extraction specificity is an under-represented contribution.**

**Where ATLAS may be original:** a head-to-head, controlled comparison of free-form English "Chain-of-Draft" prose vs DSL/schema-constrained extraction on numeric fields from financial documents, run on CPU-feasible open-weight models, with a GPU verifier in a Faithful-CoT-style architecture. Each of these elements has a paper; their combination on financial numerics for asset-manager IE does not seem to have one.

## Recommendations

**Stage 1 — Cite while shipping (now):**
1. In the project's design doc, cite GoLLIE (Sainz et al., ICLR 2024) + Code4Struct as the precedent for schema-as-Python-class extraction, and Outlines (Willard & Louf 2023) as the underlying constrained-decoding mechanism.
2. Frame the numeric-recall result against Maynez et al. 2020 (hallucinations concentrate on entities/quantities), FActScore (atomic-fact decomposition reduces hallucination), and Shao et al. 2025 ("Benford's Curse" — digit-frequency bias in FFN neurons). These three jointly explain the size of the jump.
3. Cite Faithful CoT (Lyu et al. 2023) as the architectural precedent for the extract-then-verify GPU loop.

**Stage 2 — Run before publishing (next quarter):**
1. Add a third arm to the benchmark: NL-to-Format (the Tam et al. 2024 "relaxed" condition where the model reasons in prose, then a second pass converts to schema). Required to defend against the "JSON-mode hurts reasoning" critique.
2. Test schema-order sensitivity (reasoning-field-before-answer-field vs the reverse). If the numeric-extraction gain partly comes from forcing the model to emit a verbatim copy slot *before* a derived field, that replicates and extends Tam et al.'s mechanism finding.
3. Run on at least one frontier closed-weight model (Claude / Gemini / GPT-5) for external validity, even if production deployment stays open-weight.

**Stage 3 — Publish or open-source (if results hold):**
1. The combination "CPU-feasible LLM + DSL extraction + GPU verifier in Faithful-CoT loop + financial-numeric domain" is a publishable systems contribution. Closest analogs to cite as motivation: FinDVer (EMNLP 2024), FinTagging (2025), ExtractBench (2026).
2. If the 19% → 91.7% recall replicates across 3+ open-weight models and 2+ document classes, that is a sufficient empirical magnitude to warrant either an arXiv preprint or an ACL Industry / EMNLP Industry submission.

**Benchmarks/thresholds that would change the recommendations:**
- If the structured-output gain *disappears* when the reasoning field is placed before the answer field, then the ATLAS result is primarily an instance of Tam et al.'s schema-order finding, not a new mechanism. Cite differently and de-emphasize mechanism (b).
- If the gain persists even after controlling for schema order, the cleanest explanation is mechanism (b) plus Shao et al.'s digit-bias mechanism, and the paper write-up should foreground numeric copy-mode.
- If frontier closed-weight models do not show the same magnitude of gain that qwen3:30b shows, this is a small-model-specific result (the constrained decoder is doing more work for the smaller model). Still useful, but reframe as "structured generation as a model-capability multiplier."

## Caveats

1. **The dottxt GSM8K and BFCL lift results (e.g., Meta-Llama-3-8B-Instruct from 58.73% to 84.25%) are from a non-peer-reviewed company blog**, and replication is contested by Tam et al. 2024 and Castillo's "Structured outputs can hurt the performance of LLMs" (2024). They should not be cited as primary evidence — peer-reviewed analogs (GoLLIE, Code4Struct) exist and are stronger citations.
2. **Maynez et al. 2020 is technically pre-transformer-era for the exclusion rule** (it benchmarks BERTS2S, RNNs, CNNs), but its taxonomy of intrinsic/extrinsic hallucination is the foundation of essentially all transformer-era faithfulness work and is universally cited; including it as a foundational reference is defensible.
3. **No peer-reviewed paper named "Coyote C++" or similar** appears in the financial-extraction literature; the closest peer-reviewed analogs are FinDVer (EMNLP 2024) and FinTagging (2025).
4. **Induction-heads (Olsson et al. 2022) is mechanistic-interpretability work, not extraction-benchmark work.** Treat it as a plausibility argument for the "copy mode" hypothesis, not an empirical demonstration of it.
5. **The Chain-of-Draft "5 words per step" prescription does not transfer to all task classes** — Patel et al. 2025's negative result on SWE-Bench Lite is the cautionary tale. Treat compressed CoT as task-class dependent.
6. **Several of the "structured-output-helps" results come from the .txt (Outlines) team**, which has commercial interest in this conclusion. The peer-reviewed academic results (GoLLIE, Code4Struct, PoT, PAL, FCoT) are the stronger citations.
7. **Numeric magnitudes vary by model, schema, and dataset.** The 12% (PoT), 40% (PAL on GSM-hard), 60.8% (CoS on Brick World), 78–84% token reduction (SoT), 92.6% accuracy (CoS), and 24→68 F1 (GoLLIE on Music NER) figures are headline numbers for *specific* conditions. Don't extrapolate them across conditions without re-running.
8. **Mechanistic attribution in this literature is mostly speculative**, including in this report. Most papers say "we hypothesize that…"; the only paper with controlled circuit-level evidence is Olsson et al. 2022 on induction heads, and the only paper with controlled neuron-level evidence on numerical hallucination is Shao et al. 2025.
9. **BFCL leaderboard scores cited (Llama-3.1-405B at 88.5%, Llama-3.1-70B at 84.8%)** are open-weight models on BFCL V1; closed-source comparisons across BFCL versions are not directly given by Patil et al. in the cited public summary and should be checked against the live leaderboard before publication.
